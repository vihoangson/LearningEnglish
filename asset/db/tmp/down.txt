<em>adūne</em> 'downward', from the phrase <em>of dūne</em> 'off the hill' <span class="definition"> (see <a class="word crossRef" href="  http://www.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/english/down#down-3" title="English definition of down (down3) in Oxford Dictionaries (British &amp; World English)">down<sup xmlns="">3</sup></a>)</span>.</p><div class="moreInformation"><a class="moreInformationExemples"> More </a><ul class="sentence_dictionary"><li><p class="word_origin">Downs are gently rolling hills. The word down may be of Celtic origin and related to early Irish <em>dún</em> and obsolete Welsh <em>din</em> ‘fort’, which go back to an ancient root shared by <span class="definition"><a class="word crossRef" href="  http://www.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/english/town" title="English definition of down (town) in Oxford Dictionaries (British &amp; World English)">town</a></span>. The everyday down, ‘towards a lower place’, is also Old English. It is a shortening of <b>a-down</b>, itself a reduction of <b>off down</b> ‘off the hill’. The phrase <b>down and out</b>, ‘completely without resources, defeated in life’, probably comes from boxing. It referred to a boxer who was knocked out by a blow. Since the late 19th century a <b>down-and-out</b> has been a person without money, a job, or a place to live. The fluffy down that forms the first covering of a young bird is unrelated. It came in the Middle Ages from an old Scandinavian word.</p></li></ul></div><!-- End of DIV moreInformation--></div><!-- End of DIV senseInnerWrapper--></section><section class="etymology"><div
