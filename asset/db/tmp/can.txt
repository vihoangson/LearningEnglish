class="senseInnerWrapper"><h2>Usage</h2><p>Is there any difference between <strong>may</strong> and <strong>can</strong> when used to request or express permission, as in <span class="exampleGroup exGrBreak exampleNote"> <em class="example"><strong>may/can</strong> I ask you a few questions?</em></span> Many people feel that <strong>can</strong> should be reserved for expressions denoting capability, as in <span class="exampleGroup exGrBreak exampleNote"> <em class="example"><strong>can</strong> you swim?</em></span>, rather than for those relating to permission. <strong>May</strong> is, generally speaking, a politer and more formal way of asking for something, and is the better choice in more formal contexts.</p></div><!-- End of DIV senseInnerWrapper--></section></div><!-- End of DIV --></div><!-- End of DIV --><section class="etymology"><div class="senseInnerWrapper"><h2>Origin</h2><p><span class="dateGroup"> Old English</span> <em>cunnan</em> 'know' (in <span class="dateGroup"> Middle English</span> 'know how to'), related to Dutch <em>kunnen</em> and German <em>können</em>; from an Indo-European root shared by Latin <em>gnoscere</em> 'know' and Greek <em>gignōskein</em> 'know'.</p><div class="moreInformation"><a class="moreInformationExemples"> More </a><ul class="sentence_dictionary"><li><p class="word_origin">Nowadays a <b>can</b> is a cylindrical metal container, but its ancestor, Old English <em>cann</em>, was a general word for any container for liquids. It may come from Latin <em>canna</em> (<span class="definition"> see <a class="word crossRef" href="  http://www.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/english/cannon" title="English definition of can (cannon) in Oxford Dictionaries (British &amp; World English)">cannon</a></span>). If someone <b>carries the can</b> they take responsibility for a mistake or misdeed. The origin of this expression is uncertain, but it probably started life as early 20th-century naval slang. One theory is that it refers to the beer can or keg which one sailor carried for all his companions. An early version was to <b>carry the can back</b>, which might have referred to returning the empties. In film-making and recording you can talk about something being <b>in the can</b> when it has been captured on tape or film to a satisfactory standard. Though videotape and digital recordings are not stored in cans, the older expression has been transferred to them. <span class="definition"> See also <a class="word crossRef" href="
