English</span>: from Italian, Spanish, and <a class="w translation" href="  http://www.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/english/medieval-Latin#medieval-Latin__2" title="Meaning of medieval Latin">medieval Latin</a>, from <a class="w translation" href="  http://www.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/english/Arabic#Arabic__2" title="Meaning of Arabic">Arabic</a> <em>al-jabr</em> 'the reunion of broken parts', 'bone-setting', from <em>jabara</em> 'reunite, restore'. The original sense, 'the surgical treatment of fractures', probably came via Spanish, in which it survives; the mathematical sense comes from the title of a book, <em>‘ilm al-jabr wa'l-muqābala</em> 'the science of restoring what is missing and equating like with like', by the mathematician al-Ḵwārizmī <span class="definition"> (see <a class="word crossRef" href="  http://www.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/english/algorithm" title="English definition of algebra (algorithm) in Oxford Dictionaries (British &amp; World English)">algorithm</a>)</span>.</p><div class="moreInformation"><a class="moreInformationExemples"> More </a><ul class="sentence_dictionary"><li><p class="word_origin">Bone-setting does not seem to have much to do with mathematics, but there is a connection in the word algebra. It comes from the Arabic <em>al-jabr</em> ‘the reunion of broken parts’, used specifically to refer to the surgical treatment of fractures and to bone-setting. Algebra was used in this meaning in English in the 16th century. The mathematical sense comes from the title of a 9th-century Arabic book <em>ilm al-jabr wa'l-mukabala,</em> ‘the science of restoring what is missing and equating like with like’, written by the mathematician al-Kwarizmi (<em>c</em>.790–<em>c</em>.840).</p></li></ul></div><!-- End of DIV moreInformation--></div><!-- End of DIV senseInnerWrapper--></section><div class="etymology"><div
